Let's break down exactly what the AmesburyTruth "Positive Action Lock" is in the physical world, and then translate that into high-end, secure features for your digital Pal-Lock app.

What is a "Positive Action Lock" (Physical World)?
In hardware, most windows require two steps:

You close the window.

You manually flip a switch or turn a key to lock it.

A Positive Action Lock (PAL) combines these into one step. The sheer act of closing the window automatically engages the lock. * The Philosophy: Security by default. The user doesn’t have to remember to secure themselves; the system does it for them automatically when the action is finished.

How to Build "Positive Action" into your Pal-Lock App
Since your app is built on trust (finding a plumber through your cousin, or a baker through a friend), integrating this "auto-locking" philosophy makes perfect sense, especially when money or personal data is involved.

Here are four ways you can code this concept into your app's DNA:

1. The Transaction "Auto-Lock" (For Money & Payments)
If you add a payment feature to Pal-Lock, you can use a "Positive Action" escrow system.

How it works: When you hire someone (like a trusted baker for that wedding cake) and send the money, the funds are instantly "Locked." The baker can see the money is there, but they can't withdraw it yet.

The Positive Action: The moment you press the "Job Completed" or "Window Closed" button on your screen, the lock automatically snaps open, and the money goes to the baker. It requires no extra security steps; the action of finishing the job is the lock release.

2. The "Trusted Vendor" Auto-Lock
Think about how you close a window to keep the inside safe. You can do this with people in the app.

How it works: If you search the public market for a mechanic and have a great experience with them, you close the transaction.

The Positive Action: The moment the transaction is finished, Pal-Lock automatically "locks" that mechanic into your personal trusted circle. The next time you search for a mechanic, they will show up first, alongside your friends and family. The action of a successful job automatically secures them as a trusted contact.

3. The Privacy "Snap-Shut" (Data Security)
If users are searching for sensitive things, they want to feel safe.

How it works: Let's say a user is looking for a service they want to keep private. They open a chat with a vendor.

The Positive Action: If the user switches away from the Pal-Lock app to check a text message, the Pal-Lock app instantly "closes the window" and blurs the screen. When they open the app again, it automatically requires FaceID or a fingerprint to unlock. Just like the physical window, leaving the space automatically locks it.

4. The "Handshake" Code
As a developer, you could actually name your security functions after this. When you are writing the code for transactions, you could build a function called PositiveActionLock().

It checks two things: Did the buyer get the service? Did the seller do the work? If both are true, the system auto-executes the final step securely.
